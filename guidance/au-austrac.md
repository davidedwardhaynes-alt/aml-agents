## Australia — Suspicious Matter Report (SMR) Filing Guidance

### Legal basis
- **AML/CTF Act 2006** (Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorism Financing Act)
- **AML/CTF Rules** (made under s.229)
- **Tranche 2 amendments** (passed 2024, phased rollout 2026 onwards) — extends regime to lawyers, accountants, real estate, precious metals dealers
- AUSTRAC industry-specific guidance and regulatory priorities

### Who must file
Reporting entities under the AML/CTF Act:
- Authorised deposit-taking institutions (banks, credit unions, building societies, neobanks)
- Insurers, life insurance product providers
- Designated remittance providers (remitters, money changers)
- Gambling service providers (casinos, online wagering, bookmakers)
- Bullion dealers
- **Digital currency exchange providers (DCEs)** — registered with AUSTRAC
- Securities and derivatives dealers (ASIC-licensed)
- **Tranche 2 entities** (from 2026, phased): lawyers, accountants, conveyancers, real estate agents, precious metals/stones dealers

### Tranche 2 reforms — phased rollout

The **AML/CTF Amendment Act 2024** extended the AML/CTF Act to Tranche 2 entities. Key dates:

- **Royal Assent**: November 2024
- **Phase 1 (1 March 2026)**: pre-commencement registration window opens for Tranche 2 entities
- **Phase 2 (1 July 2026)**: full obligations commence — CDD, TM, SMR, record-keeping
- **Sectoral phasing** (per AUSTRAC industry guidance): legal practitioners, accountants, real estate agents, conveyancers, and precious metals/stones dealers all in scope from 2026

Tranche 2 entity practical implications:
- First-ever AML/CTF Programs required (Part A & Part B)
- AML/CTF Compliance Officer appointment mandatory
- Customer source-of-wealth diligence at engagement
- SMR obligation under s.41 — note legal-professional-privilege carve-out is narrow
- AUSTRAC has stated enforcement leniency in the first 12 months for genuine implementation efforts but no immunity for wilful non-compliance

### AUSTRAC current enforcement priorities (2025–2026)

Per AUSTRAC's published priorities and industry briefings:

1. **Investment scams / "Pig Butchering" / romance scams** — AUD 2.7B+ stolen from Australians in 2024; focus on mule-account detection at ADIs and DCEs
2. **Child sexual exploitation funding** — payments to high-risk jurisdictions
3. **Sanctions evasion** — particularly Russia / Iran / DPRK exposure; DFAT consolidated list compliance
4. **Casino sector compliance** — post-Star (AUD 100M+ penalty) and Crown (AUD 450M+ penalty) enforcement actions; ongoing monitoring
5. **Tranche 2 readiness** — capability uplift across legal, accounting, real estate sectors
6. **Wagering sector** — scam-mule deposits and source-of-wealth in online wagering

### Threshold for filing
"Suspects on **reasonable grounds**" that information about a person, the matter, or a transaction may be relevant to:
- investigation or prosecution of an offence against any Commonwealth, State, or Territory law
- assistance to a foreign country investigating an equivalent offence
- the provisions of the AML/CTF Act, FTR Act, or POC Act

The matter does NOT need to involve actual proceeds of crime — it's enough that the matter is relevant to an investigation.

### Timing
- **Money laundering / general: within 3 business days** of forming the suspicion
- **Terrorism financing: within 24 hours** of forming the suspicion
- **Threshold transaction reports (TTR)** for cash ≥ AUD 10,000: separate, also 10 business days

### Filing channel
**AUSTRAC Online** — secure portal at online.austrac.gov.au.

Reporting entities enrol with AUSTRAC to receive credentials. Most SMRs filed via the online form; high-volume entities use file uploads in AUSTRAC's prescribed XML schema.

### Required content
1. Reporting entity details
2. Suspect / customer identification
3. Beneficial ownership (where applicable)
4. Transaction details (or attempted transaction) — date, amount, currency, channel
5. Description of the suspicion (the narrative)
6. Indicators / red flags identified
7. Action taken — including whether the transaction was completed or refused
8. Supporting evidence (CDD records, statements, communications)

### Tipping-off (s.123 AML/CTF Act)
**Strict prohibition.** A reporting entity (or person) must not disclose to anyone that:
- an SMR has been or will be made
- information relating to the matter has been given to AUSTRAC

Limited exceptions exist for internal AML/CTF Program purposes and for legal proceedings.

**Penalty (individual):** up to **2 years** imprisonment, **120 penalty units** (~AUD 39,600 at 2025 rate of AUD 330/unit).
**Penalty (corporate):** up to **600 penalty units** (~AUD 198,000) per offence.

### Record retention
**Minimum 7 years** from creation date — SMR records, CDD records, transaction records, AML/CTF Program documents.

### Penalties (non-compliance)
- Failure to lodge SMR (s.41): up to **2,000 penalty units** for individuals (~AUD 660,000), **10,000 penalty units** for body corporate (~AUD 3.3 million) per offence
- Civil penalty regime — AUSTRAC has historically imposed multi-million-dollar penalties (e.g., Westpac AUD 1.3 billion, CBA AUD 700 million) for systemic AML failures
- AUSTRAC enforcement actions: enforceable undertakings, infringement notices, Federal Court proceedings

### Special considerations
- **Designated services list** — only transactions involving "designated services" trigger reporting obligations. Check whether the activity is in scope.
- **International funds transfer instructions (IFTI)** — separate reporting obligation for cross-border transfers, regardless of suspicion.
- **Cryptocurrency** — DCEs must report SMRs the same way; Tranche 2 expansion adds new entity types from 2026.
- **AUSTRAC's "key risks" priorities** updated annually — align internal red-flag indicators to the current focus areas (e.g., scams, child exploitation, sanctions evasion).
