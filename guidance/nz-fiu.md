## New Zealand — SAR Filing Guidance

### Legal basis
- **AML/CFT Act 2009** — Anti-Money Laundering and Countering Financing of Terrorism Act 2009
- **AML/CFT Regulations** (multiple — including the Definitions Regulations, Requirements and Compliance Regulations, Cross-Border Transportation of Cash Regulations, and Class Exemptions)
- **Terrorism Suppression Act 2002** — terrorism-financing offences
- **Crimes Act 1961** — predicate offence anchors (ss.243-245 money-laundering offences)
- **Misuse of Drugs Act 1975** — drug-related predicate offences
- **Tax Administration Act 1994** — tax-evasion predicate offence
- Supervisor sectoral guidance: **RBNZ AML/CFT Sectoral Guidance** (banks, NBDTs, life insurers); **FMA AML/CFT Sectoral Guidance** (issuers, managers, advisers, custodians); **DIA AML/CFT Sectoral Guidance** (casinos, money remitters, lawyers/conveyancers, accountants, real-estate agents, dealers in high-value goods, TCSPs)

### Who must file
**RBNZ-supervised:**
- Registered banks
- Non-bank deposit takers (NBDTs)
- Life insurers

**FMA-supervised:**
- Issuers, fund managers, derivatives issuers
- Financial advisers and Financial Advice Provider licensees
- Custodians, DIMS providers
- Equity crowdfunding and peer-to-peer lending services

**DIA-supervised:**
- Casinos (SkyCity Auckland, Christchurch Casino, Dunedin Casino, Hamilton Casino, Queenstown Casinos)
- Money remitters and currency exchanges
- Lawyers and conveyancers (when conducting captured activities)
- Accountants (when conducting captured activities)
- Real-estate agents
- Dealers in high-value goods (DHVGs — when accepting cash ≥ NZD 10,000)
- Trust and corporate-service providers (TCSPs)

### Threshold for filing — SAR
"Reasonable grounds to suspect" (s.40 of the Act) that a transaction or proposed transaction or activity is or may be relevant to:
- An investigation into a money-laundering offence (Crimes Act ss.243-245)
- An investigation into the financing of terrorism (Terrorism Suppression Act)
- The enforcement of the Misuse of Drugs Act
- The enforcement of any offence punishable by 12 months' imprisonment or more

### Timing
- File **as soon as practicable** after suspicion is formed; AML/CFT Regulations specify within **3 working days**.
- For terrorism financing, file **immediately** under the Terrorism Suppression Act and freeze the funds.
- PTR (Prescribed Transaction Report) timing is separate — see below.

### Filing channel
**FIU NZ** — within the New Zealand Police. SARs and PTRs are filed through **goAML**, the UNODC's reporting platform deployed by FIU NZ. Reporting entities register with FIU NZ, nominate an AML/CFT Compliance Officer (s.56 of the Act), and are issued goAML credentials.

### Required content
1. Reporting entity identification — name, supervisor (RBNZ / FMA / DIA), AML/CFT Compliance Officer
2. Customer (natural or juridical) — identity verification record (s.14-15 standard CDD), beneficial owner (per AML/CFT Regulations definition)
3. Transaction or activity giving rise to suspicion — date, channel, amount, counterparty
4. Reasons for suspicion — narrative referencing the relevant Crimes Act / Terrorism Suppression Act / Misuse of Drugs Act predicate offence(s) and any matching FIU NZ typology
5. Action taken by the reporting entity

### PTR (Prescribed Transaction Reports) — separate from SAR
A reporting entity must file a PTR for:
- **Cash transactions ≥ NZD 10,000** in a single transaction
- **International wire transfers ≥ NZD 1,000**

Both thresholds apply per transaction. PTRs are filed via goAML on a near-real-time basis (the operational standard is within 10 working days, but supervisors expect faster). PTRs do not substitute for an SAR — both must be filed where suspicion exists.

### Predicate offences (Crimes Act + related — abbreviated)
- Money laundering (Crimes Act ss.243-245)
- Drug trafficking (Misuse of Drugs Act 1975)
- Terrorism and terrorism financing (Terrorism Suppression Act 2002)
- Tax evasion (Tax Administration Act 1994)
- Fraud and dishonesty offences (Crimes Act Part 10)
- Bribery and corruption (Crimes Act ss.99-105)
- Smuggling and customs offences
- Gambling Act offences
- Trafficking in persons
- Identity-related crimes
- Any other offence punishable by 12 months' imprisonment or more

### Tipping-off
s.46 of the AML/CFT Act prohibits the reporting entity and its officers, employees, and agents from disclosing the fact of an SAR or PTR to the customer or to any unauthorised party. Customer communications about transaction holds, EDD requests, or account closures must comply.

### Penalties for non-filing
The Act prescribes civil and criminal penalties for non-compliance:
- Civil penalties up to NZD 200,000 for natural persons and NZD 2 million for body corporates per breach
- Criminal penalties (s.91-93) up to 2 years' imprisonment and/or NZD 300,000 / NZD 5 million for officers and entities respectively

### Practical tips
- FIU NZ publishes the annual *National Risk Assessment* and quarterly *Typology Reports* — incorporate the typology reference where the case fits (e.g., the Auckland real-estate cash-buyer typology, the Pacific-Islands TBML corridor typology).
- For trans-Tasman law and accounting firms with Australian Tranche 2 obligations, both the AUSTRAC SMR and the FIU NZ SAR may be required for the same matter — file both, cross-referencing.
- Real-estate cases: DIA's sectoral guidance has explicit red-flag indicators on Auckland and Queenstown high-value-property transactions.
- Crypto: digital-currency exchange and remittance providers fall under DIA supervision; the SAR + PTR obligation applies even where the customer is offshore but transacts via the NZ-licensed platform.
